What is Typhoon?

A typhoon is a large, powerful and violent tropical cyclone. It is a low-pressure area rotating counter-clockwise and containing rising warm air that forms over warm water in the Western Pacific Ocean. Less powerful tropical cyclones are called Tropical Depressions and Tropical Storms. A typhoon is called a hurricane in the Atlantic Ocean, a cyclone in the Indian Ocean and wily-wily in Australia. Typhoons can inflict terrible damage due to thunderstorms, violent winds, torrential rain, floods, landslides, large and very big waves associated with storm surges.

Classifications of Typhoons

Tropical Depression - maximum winds from 35 kph to 63 kph

Tropical Storm - maximum winds from 64 kph to 118 kph

Typhoons - maximum winds exceeding 118 kph

Typhoon Preparedness

Before the Typhoon

  • Monitor the news about weather disturbances. As the typhoon nears, watch out for the latest severe weather bulletin issued by PAGASA every six hours.
  • Board up windows or put storm shutters.
  • Check the ability of your house to withstand strong winds; strengthen it if necessary.
  • For fisherfolks, place boats in safe areas.
  • For farmers, harvest crops that can already be yielded.
  • Secure domesticated animals in a safe place.
  • Make sure flashlights and battery-powered radios have fresh batteries or are electrically-charged; stock up on candles and matches.
  • Store adequate food supply and clean water. Have food that do not require cooking.
  • Ready your emergency kit/ grab bag to include some first aid kit, candles/flashlight, clothes, food, and radio.
  • Prepare a list of emergency hotline numbers, including contact details of authorities.
  • Be on standby and assess when it may be necessary for you to evacuate to safer areas.
  • Secure important documents.

    • During the Typhoon

  • Continue to monitor the news for weather updates.

    • For public storm signal number 1 or 2

  • People travelling by sea and air must avoid unnecessary risks.
  • Evacuate from low-lying areas to higher grounds.

    • In case of storm signal number 3 or 4

  • Watch out for the passage of the "eye" of the storm.
  • Stay inside safe and strong houses or structures or proceed to evacuation centers.
  • Stay away from coasts and riverbanks.
  • Cancel all travels and outdoor activities.

    • If asked by authorities to evacuate, do so calmly; shut all windows, turn off main power supply, and secure the house.

    After the Typhoon

  • Do not touch or approach any live wires or outlets immersed with water.
  • Check whether the water supply is safe to drink. If not, boil water for at least 20 minutes.
  • Keep all power and electronic appliances off until cleared safe by a professional.
  • Beware of any dangerous animals i.e., snake, that may have entered your house.
  • Call power utility companies if there are any damaged transmission lines or posts.
  • If your house is insured, take photos of the extent of damage to your house to support your insurance claims.
  • Clean the entire home and all objects affected by flood as chemicals and sewage water may have contaminated these. Wear appropriate gear (gloves & mask).
  • If you have evacuated, only return to your house after authorities deem it safe.
  • If your house was damaged or destroyed, make sure that it is safe and stable before entering.
